## Visual Pathways and Phototransduction

**Q1.** Which of the following is true of vision and phototransduction?
- A. Damage to the optic chiasm will cause bitemporal hemianopia
- B. GMP is converted to cGMP by phosphodiesterase
- C. Transducin is a G-protein
- D. Na+ channels open when rhodopsin's retinal is converted to its all-trans form

**Answer: A**
Damage to the optic chiasm affects crossing nasal fibers, leading to loss of the temporal visual fields (bitemporal hemianopia). Transducin is indeed a G-protein, but A is the primary clinical defect associated with chiasm lesions. Note that Na+ channels actually CLOSE in light.

---

**Q2.** Michelle cuts the fibers of Larry's left nasal optic nerve before the optic chiasm. What visual field defect results?
- A. Loss of vision in left visual field in both eyes
- B. Loss of vision in right visual field in both eyes
- C. Loss of vision in left visual field in left eye
- D. Loss of vision in right visual field in left eye

**Answer: D**
Nasal retinal fibers carry information from the temporal visual field. Cutting the left nasal fibers eliminates the left eye's temporal field, which corresponds to the right visual field of that specific eye.

---

**Q3.** Which best describes the underlying optical defect in astigmatism?
- A. The lens loses its elasticity and cannot accommodate for near vision
- B. The cornea or lens has an irregular curvature, causing light to focus at multiple points
- C. The eyeball is too short, causing light to focus behind the retina
- D. Increased intraocular pressure damages the optic nerve

**Answer: B**
Astigmatism occurs when the cornea or lens is not perfectly spherical, leading to blurred vision at all distances because light is refracted unevenly.

**Q4.** Which option correctly describes the pathway of visual information from the retina to the brain?
- A. Retina → Optic chiasm → Optic nerve → LGN → Visual cortex
- B. Retina → Optic nerve → Optic chiasm → Optic tract → LGN → Visual cortex
- C. Retina → LGN → Optic nerve → Optic tract → Visual cortex
- D. Retina → Optic tract → Optic chiasm → Visual cortex → LGN

**Answer: B**
The correct anatomical sequence is Retina → Optic Nerve → Optic Chiasm → Optic Tract → LGN → Visual Cortex.

**Q5.** The principal neurotransmitter released by photoreceptors in the dark, and its effect on ON-bipolar cells:
- A. Glutamate; sign-inverting (via mGluR6) — hyperpolarizes ON-bipolar cells in the dark
- B. GABA; directly inhibits bipolar cells
- C. Glycine; depolarizes ON-bipolar cells
- D. Acetylcholine; activates nicotinic receptors on bipolar cells

**Answer: A**
In the dark, photoreceptors are depolarized and continuously release glutamate. For ON-bipolar cells, glutamate is inhibitory (sign-inverting) via mGluR6 receptors.

**Q6.** A lesion of Meyer's loop (inferior fibers of the optic radiation in the temporal lobe) causes:
- A. Contralateral superior quadrantanopia
- B. Contralateral inferior quadrantanopia
- C. Ipsilateral superior quadrantanopia
- D. Bitemporal hemianopia

**Answer: A**
Meyer's loop carries information from the superior visual world. A lesion here causes a "pie in the sky" defect, or contralateral superior quadrantanopia.

**Q7.** During dark adaptation, photoreceptors exhibit:
- A. Low cGMP; Na+/Ca2+ channels closed; hyperpolarized
- B. High cGMP; Na+/Ca2+ channels open; depolarized
- C. High cAMP; K+ channels open; hyperpolarized
- D. Low Ca2+; glutamate release decreased

**Answer: B**
In the dark, cGMP levels are high, keeping cyclic nucleotide-gated channels open. This allows the "dark current" (influx of Na+ and Ca2+) to keep the cell depolarized.

## Olfactory and Gustatory Systems

**Q14.** Which second messenger system is primarily involved in olfactory signal transduction?
- A. IP3 pathway
- B. cAMP pathway involving Golf protein
- C. cGMP pathway
- D. JAK-STAT pathway

**Answer: B**
Olfactory receptors are GPCRs that activate a specific G-protein called Golf, which stimulates adenylyl cyclase to produce cAMP.

---

**Q15.** Which statement about the olfactory pathway is uniquely correct compared to other special senses?
- A. Projects first to thalamus
- B. Regenerating receptor neurons from basal cells
- C. Uses primarily glycine as its neurotransmitter
- D. Travels exclusively in CN V

**Answer: B**
Olfactory neurons are unique because they are regularly replaced by basal stem cells. Additionally, olfaction is the only special sense that bypasses the thalamus on its way to the primary cortex.

---

**Q16.** Which cranial nerve carries taste from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue?
- A. CN IX
- B. CN VII
- C. CN X
- D. CN V3

**Answer: B**
The Facial Nerve (CN VII) carries taste from the anterior 2/3 of the tongue via the chorda tympani branch.

## Vestibular and Auditory Systems

**Q23.** The utricle detects ____, the saccule detects ____, and the semicircular canals detect ____.
- A. Angular; Vertical; Horizontal
- B. Angular; Horizontal; Vertical
- C. Vertical; Horizontal; Angular
- D. Horizontal; Vertical; Angular

**Answer: D**
The utricle senses horizontal linear acceleration, the saccule senses vertical linear acceleration, and the semicircular canals sense angular (rotational) acceleration.

**Q24.** Organ of Corti hair cell depolarization occurs when stereocilia deflect:
- A. Toward shortest cilium → K+ efflux
- B. Toward tallest cilium → K+ influx from endolymph
- C. Toward tallest cilium → Na+ influx from perilymph
- D. Toward shortest cilium → Ca2+ influx from endolymph

**Answer: B**
Deflection toward the kinocilium (tallest) opens tip-links, allowing K+ from the high-potassium endolymph to enter and depolarize the cell.

**Q25.** Which part of the cochlea responds to high-frequency sounds?
- A. Base (near oval window)
- B. Apex
- C. Middle turn
- D. Helicotrema

**Answer: A**
The basilar membrane is stiff and narrow at the base, making it resonate at high frequencies. It is wide and floppy at the apex, which responds to low frequencies.

## Spinal Cord and Proprioception

**Q30.** A patient suffers a complete right-sided hemisection at T4 (Brown-Séquard syndrome). What is expected below T4?
- A. Loss of all sensation bilaterally; preserved motor function
- B. Loss of pain/temp on left; loss of fine touch/proprioception and voluntary motor on right
- C. Loss of pain/temp on right; loss of fine touch/proprioception and voluntary motor on left
- D. Loss of all sensation and motor on the left

**Answer: B**
In Brown-Séquard, motor and DCML (touch) loss is ipsilateral (same side) because they cross in the medulla. Spinothalamic (pain/temp) loss is contralateral (opposite side) because it crosses in the spinal cord.

---

**Q31.** Syringomyelia presents with a "cape-like" loss of pain and temperature. Why?
- A. Dorsal columns are most resistant to compression
- B. Fine touch fibers are myelinated and immune to central cavitation
- C. The crossing spinothalamic fibers at the anterior commissure are disrupted first
- D. The syrinx forms posteriorly, sparing anterior spinothalamic fibers

**Answer: C**
A syrinx (cavity) in the center of the cord hits the fibers crossing the anterior white commissure first. These are the spinothalamic fibers, leading to bilateral loss of pain and temp at those segments.

**Q32.** Muscle spindles detect ____, while Golgi tendon organs detect ____.
- A. Tension; Length
- B. Length/Stretch; Tension/Force
- C. Vibration; Pressure
- D. Heat; Pain

**Answer: B**
Spindles are in parallel with muscle fibers and detect stretch. GTOs are in series at the tendon and detect tension/contraction force.

**Q33.** Upper motor neuron (UMN) lesions produce:
- A. Flaccid paralysis, muscle atrophy, fasciculations, absent reflexes
- B. Spastic paralysis, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign, minimal atrophy
- C. Flaccid paralysis, hyperreflexia, intact sensation
- D. Spastic paralysis, absent reflexes, sensory loss

**Answer: B**
UMN lesions (like stroke or MS) remove descending inhibition, leading to "overactive" muscles (spasticity and hyperreflexia). LMN lesions lead to "dead" muscles (flaccidity and atrophy).

**Q34.** What is the difference between Aδ and C nerve fibers? *(3 pts)*

**Q45.** A person reflexively pulls their foot away after stepping on a nail, then consciously decides to look down and move. Which response occurs faster, and why? *(4 pts)*
**Answer:** The reflex withdrawal is faster. It travels only to the spinal cord (not the brain) via a polysynaptic reflex arc, bypassing cortical processing. ## Endocrine System

**Q46.** Which hormone acts on nuclear receptors?
- A. Insulin
- B. PTH
- C. Cortisol
- D. TSH

**Answer: C**
Cortisol is a steroid hormone. Steroids are lipid-soluble and travel through the cell membrane to bind receptors inside the nucleus to alter gene expression.

**Q47.** Which endocrine organ stores hormone-containing colloid extracellularly?
- A. Adrenal cortex

The thyroid is unique in storing vast amounts of precursor hormone (thyroglobulin) in extracellular follicles called colloid.

# PART II: CASE STUDIES

## Nervous System Cases

### Case 1 — ALS *(SOAPS Invitational)*
A 50-year-old man presents to his primary care physician reporting difficulty with fine hand movements, twitching in his arms, weakness in his ankles, a propensity for tripping and falling, and some unexpected changes to his emotional state. He has had no issues with bladder control, sensory degeneration, and does not report pain associated with muscle weakness. He has a history of regular smoking and a career in military service.

**(a)** What disease does this patient have? *(2 pts)*

**(b)** Describe the physiological effects of this disease. *(4 pts)*

**(c)** List two treatment options. *(2 pts)*

**Answer Key:**
- (a) ALS (Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis)
- (b) Progressive degeneration of both UMN and LMN: UMN loss → spasticity, hyperreflexia, Babinski sign; LMN loss → fasciculations, muscle atrophy, flaccid weakness; bulbar involvement → dysphagia, dysarthria; sensory and bladder function preserved
- (c) Riluzole (glutamate antagonist, slows progression); Edaravone (antioxidant); supportive/palliative care (ventilatory support, PEG tube)

---

### Case 2 — ALS with UMN/LMN Classification *(UCR Highlander Invitational)*
A 61-year-old male speaks slowly and reports left-sided hand weakness, difficulty walking, and tongue fasciculations. On exam: left forearm atrophy, depressed left arm reflexes, right leg hypertonia. His doctor mentions a positive "Ba-something" sign.

**(a)** What disease does he have? *(1 pt)*

**(b)** Name all relevant symptoms from the passage. Classify each as a UMN or LMN sign. *(4 pts — all or nothing)*

**(c)** What is the "positive Ba-something sign"? *(2 pts)*

### Case 5 — Auditory Processing Disorder *(UMSO Invitational)*
A 10-year-old girl struggles to follow instructions in class and did not improve when moved to the front row. ### Case 14 — Hashimoto's Thyroiditis with Hashitoxicosis *(UMSO Invitational)*

**(a)** List key signs/symptoms from the initial visit suggesting a thyroid disorder. How do these relate to thyroid hormone activity? *(4 pts)*

**(d)** Explain a physiological mechanism relevant to the diagnosis that accounts for the earlier hyperthyroid presentation. *(4 pts)*

**Answer Key:**

### Case 15 — Addison's Disease (Acute Crisis Precipitated by Infection) *(UMSO Invitational)*

- (c) Hyponatremia (aldosterone deficiency → Na⁺ wasting); hyperkalemia (aldosterone deficiency → K⁺ retention); hypoglycemia (cortisol deficiency → impaired gluconeogenesis)
